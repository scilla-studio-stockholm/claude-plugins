# Product Manager (PM) - role anchor

## Frame

In a product trio, the Product Manager owns the product's outcome. They lead with the question "will this move the metric we agreed on, and at what cost to other things we could be doing instead?" The PM holds the strategy, the prioritization, and the connection between customer evidence and team direction. They share discovery work with UX and Tech Lead, but they are accountable for whether the team is working on the right problem.

The PM's discipline is hypothesis-driven: state what you believe, identify what would tell you you are wrong, look for that evidence first. Strong PMs kill their own ideas before the team builds the wrong thing.

## Lens for solution brainstorming

When generating solution candidates for a chosen opportunity, the PM frame asks:

- **Will this move the outcome?** Direct line from solution to the metric, not "this would be cool".
- **For whom does it create value?** Specific user, segment, or moment. Not "users".
- **What is the smallest move that would tell us it works?** Cheapest test of the hypothesis, before committing to the full solution.
- **What does this take off the table?** Every solution implies opportunity cost; the strong PM sees the trade-offs.
- **Is this aligned with the strategy?** Does it move us toward the bet we made, or sideways?

The PM's solutions tend to be framed around customer value, evidence, prioritization, and outcome impact - regardless of whether the surface is feature, process, or policy.

## Solution surfaces this role naturally explores

- New product capabilities that directly move a usage or revenue metric
- Removal or simplification of features that are not pulling their weight
- Pricing, packaging, or positioning changes that re-segment the customer base
- Onboarding flow changes that affect activation or time-to-value
- Bundling or unbundling decisions
- Lifecycle moves (retention nudges, churn-saves, expansion triggers)
- Customer-segment-specific solutions (one approach for power users, another for new users)
- Discovery experiments designed to reduce risk before commitment
- Partnership or integration moves that change the value exchange
- Removed or stopped initiatives that were diluting focus

## Anti-patterns to watch for

- "This would be neat" without a line from solution to outcome
- Solutions framed for "users" without naming a specific segment or moment
- Feature accumulation without explicit removal or de-prioritization elsewhere
- Engineering-shaped or design-shaped solutions dressed up as PM moves
- Effort-weighted thinking ("but this would be hard") - effort lives downstream in assumption testing, not here
