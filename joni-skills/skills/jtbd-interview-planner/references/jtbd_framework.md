# Jobs To Be Done Interview Framework

Reference for structuring JTBD-focused customer interviews. This framework uncovers the causal mechanisms behind customer behavior—not just what they did, but why, and what progress they were trying to make.

## Core JTBD Principles

### The Job, Not the Product

Customers don't buy products. They hire them to make progress in their lives. The interview uncovers:
- What progress were they trying to make?
- What circumstances triggered the search?
- What were they firing (old solution) to hire something new?

### The Timeline

Every purchase/adoption has a story arc. The interview reconstructs this timeline:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  FIRST        PASSIVE       ACTIVE        DECIDING      CONSUMING       │
│  THOUGHT      LOOKING       LOOKING                                     │
│                                                                         │
│  "Something   Noticing      Actively      Weighing      Using the      │
│  isn't        alternatives  searching     options,      solution,      │
│  working"     in passing    and           making        experiencing   │
│                             evaluating    tradeoffs     outcomes       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Forces of Progress

Four forces determine whether someone switches to a new solution:

**Forces pushing toward change:**
1. **Push of the current situation** - Pain, frustration, limitations of status quo
2. **Pull of the new solution** - Attraction, appeal, imagined better future

**Forces opposing change:**
3. **Anxiety of the new** - Fear of unknown, switching costs, learning curve
4. **Habit of the present** - Comfort, familiarity, "good enough" inertia

A switch happens only when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit

### The Struggling Moment

The most important moment to uncover: when did the customer first realize something wasn't working? This is the seed of all demand.

**Characteristics of struggling moments:**
- Specific, concrete, situational
- Emotionally charged (frustration, embarrassment, fear)
- Often involves other people or external pressure
- Has a "last straw" quality

## Interview Arc Structure

### Phase 1: Establish the Timeline (5-7 min)

**Goal:** Anchor to a specific instance. Avoid generalities.

Key question: *"Take me back to when you first started thinking about [solving this problem / finding a solution / changing how you did X]. What was going on?"*

Listen for:
- Specific dates, events, circumstances
- Other people involved
- What triggered the thought

**If they jump to the solution:** "Back up a bit—before you found [solution], what was happening that made you start looking?"

### Phase 2: First Thought / Struggling Moment (8-10 min)

**Goal:** Uncover the push—what wasn't working.

Key questions:
- *"What was happening in your [work/life] that made you realize something needed to change?"*
- *"Can you think of a specific moment when you thought 'this isn't working'?"*
- *"What were you trying to do when you hit that wall?"*

Listen for:
- Functional struggles (can't do X, takes too long)
- Emotional struggles (frustrated, embarrassed, anxious)
- Social struggles (looks bad to others, team pressure)

**Probe the emotion:** "You said you were frustrated—what made it frustrating specifically?"

### Phase 3: Passive Looking (5-7 min)

**Goal:** Understand what alternatives they were aware of before actively searching.

Key questions:
- *"Before you started seriously looking, what options were you already aware of?"*
- *"Had you heard of any solutions? Where?"*
- *"What did you think of those options at the time?"*

Listen for:
- Awareness sources (colleagues, ads, content, reviews)
- Initial impressions and misconceptions
- Why they hadn't switched yet (anxiety, habit)

### Phase 4: Active Looking / Event Trigger (8-10 min)

**Goal:** Find what pushed them from passive to active—the triggering event.

Key questions:
- *"What happened that made you actually start looking seriously?"*
- *"Was there a specific event or moment that pushed you to act?"*
- *"What changed between knowing about options and actually searching?"*

Listen for:
- External triggers (deadline, new project, team change)
- Internal triggers (breaking point, new information)
- The "last straw"

**Probe the trigger:** "You mentioned [trigger]—tell me more about that. What specifically happened?"

### Phase 5: Deciding / Tradeoffs (8-10 min)

**Goal:** Understand the decision criteria and what they gave up.

Key questions:
- *"What options did you seriously consider?"*
- *"How did you evaluate them? What mattered most?"*
- *"What concerns did you have about [chosen solution]?"*
- *"What made you ultimately decide?"*

Listen for:
- Decision criteria (functional, emotional, social)
- Anxieties about the new solution
- What they were willing to sacrifice
- Who else influenced the decision

**Probe tradeoffs:** "You chose [X] even though [concern]—how did you think about that tradeoff?"

### Phase 6: Consuming / Outcomes (5-7 min)

**Goal:** Validate whether the job got done and what progress looked like.

Key questions:
- *"Now that you've been using it, has it delivered what you hoped?"*
- *"What's different now compared to before?"*
- *"What surprised you—good or bad?"*
- *"Is there anything it hasn't solved?"*

Listen for:
- Progress made (functional, emotional, social)
- Unmet expectations
- New jobs that emerged
- Potential for expansion or churn

## The Four Forces in Practice

### Uncovering Push (Current Situation Pain)

- *"What wasn't working about how you were doing it before?"*
- *"What was the hardest part about [old way]?"*
- *"If nothing had changed, what would have happened?"*

### Uncovering Pull (New Solution Attraction)

- *"What caught your attention about [solution]?"*
- *"What did you imagine would be different?"*
- *"What was the dream scenario you were hoping for?"*

### Uncovering Anxiety (Fear of New)

- *"What concerns did you have before switching?"*
- *"What made you hesitate?"*
- *"What could have gone wrong?"*

### Uncovering Habit (Inertia of Old)

- *"What was working about the old way?"*
- *"What did you have to give up by switching?"*
- *"Was there anything you missed about how you did it before?"*

## Red Flags During Interview

| Red Flag | What It Means | How to Redirect |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Speaking in generalities | Not anchored to specific instance | "Can you think of a specific time when...?" |
| Jumping to features | Rationalizing, not remembering | "Back up—before you knew about [feature], what were you trying to do?" |
| "I just needed X" | Surface-level, missing context | "What was happening that made you need X right then?" |
| Perfect recall of decision | Likely post-hoc rationalization | "What concerns did you have at the time?" |
| No emotion in story | Missing the struggling moment | "Was there a moment when you felt frustrated or stuck?" |
| Talking about others | Avoiding their own experience | "What about for you personally?" |

## Output: What Good Looks Like

A successful JTBD interview produces:

1. **A clear struggling moment** - Specific, emotional, situational
2. **The timeline reconstructed** - First thought → trigger → search → decide → use
3. **All four forces identified** - Push, pull, anxiety, habit with evidence
4. **The job statement** - "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"
5. **Competitive context** - What they fired, what else they considered
6. **Quotable moments** - Specific, vivid quotes that capture the experience

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Asking about the product too early** - Stay in their world, their problem, their life
2. **Accepting the first answer** - The real insight is usually 2-3 "whys" deep
3. **Leading toward your hypothesis** - Let their story emerge
4. **Skipping the emotion** - Functional answers are incomplete; probe for feeling
5. **Not anchoring to specifics** - "Usually" and "typically" are useless; find the instance
6. **Rushing the timeline** - The sequence matters; don't let them skip steps
